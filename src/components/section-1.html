The PIN-entering interface

Our interface is designed for a user to type a 4-digit PIN. It is like when you go to an ATM and are asked to type your secret PIN. The only difference is that instead of typing directly on your digit, you have to tell the machine what color is your digit.

The interface (Figure 1) is showing all digits from 0 to 9 each colored in one of two colors (yellow or grey). The machine is asking: "What color is your digit?". You look at the digit you want to enter and answer: "My digit is yellow/grey" by clicking on the button with the corresponding color.

For example, in Figure 1, the machine asks you the color of the first digit of your PINAs indicated by the dark frame around the left most digit in the top of Figure 1. If you wanted to type a '1', you would click on the grey button because the digit '1' is colored in grey.

Because the machine knows the colors applied on the digitsCurious minds might be asking themselves how the machine decides what colors to apply on the digits. It is indeed a very stimulating problem but let's put it on the side for now and pretend we live in a world where the colors magically appear on the digits. We will discuss planning in section 5., when you click on the grey button, it can immediately discard all the yellow ones as the digit you have in mind. By repeating the process 3 or 4 times, it can identify with certainty your first digit, and move to the second one.

You now fully understand the principle of interaction between you and this machine. If you have a background in computer science, you probably know how to implement this at home. But before moving to the self-calibrating version of this interface, let us name the various components at play and visually explain how this version works for everyone to understand.

First, we should name three distinct components:

  • The user's intent is what the user wants the machine to do. Here entering a specific digit.
  • The user's meaning is what the user wants to say to the machine. Here it is either: "My digit is yellow" or "My digit is grey".
  • The user's action is what the user does in order to express their meaning. Here user's actions are to press buttons.

In short, an action conveys a meaning that is used to infer an intent. But infering the intent from a meaning requires a bit of context. In our case, the context is a user wanting to type a PIN via our interface. Hence we know:

  • The user aims to type a PIN one digit at a time. Thus their intent is one of the ten possible digits.
  • The user follows the principle of interaction of providing feedback about the color of the digit they want to type. Thus, the possible meanings are yellow or grey.
  • The user can perform one of two actions, pressing either the left or the right button.
  • The mapping between the user's actions and their meanings. Pressing the left button conveys the meaning yellow and the right button conveys the meaning grey.

The latter is the most important element of our story, it explicits that there is a pre-existing shared understanding between the user and the machine on the meaning conveyed by the pressing of each buttons. It is that assumption that we will relax in the next section.

But for now the machine can follow this simple reasoning: "If the user presses the yellow button, then we can discard all the grey digits". To be precise, we should better say: "If the user presses the left button, then they mean that their digit is currently yellow, thus we can discard all the grey digits."
