Interfaces are everywhere. They enable us to convey our intent to machines. Typically, buttons are to be pressed and the letters printed on them indicate what they do. But are the letters always necessary? In this article, we demonstrate how a machine can respond appropriately to users' inputs without knowing exactly what they mean.

To explore this question we designed a PIN-entering interface. You will use it to type a 4-digit PIN of your choice. But instead of pressing directly on each digits, like at an ATM, you will rather press colored buttons that inform the machine of the color currently associated to your digit. The machine will be able to identify your PIN via a process of elimination. Which will quickly look like magic when colors won't be pre-assigned to each button, but instead assigned by you on the fly and undisclosed to the machine.

Before providing an overview of this article, we think you should first try the interface.

The interface can be broken down into three parts. The top part is where your 4-digit PIN will be displayed. The middle part shows all digits from 0 to 9 colored in either yellow or grey. The bottom part is for user interaction, for now it contains two buttons, one yellow and one grey. The machine is asking you: "What color is your digit?". Looking at the digit you want to enter, you answer: "My digit is yellow/grey" by clicking on the corresponding button.

For example, if your PIN is 1234. You would start by typing the first digit of our PINAs indicated by the dark frame around the left most digit in the top of Figure 1, that is a 1. Because the digit 1 is currently colored in yellow, you click on the yellow button. The machine can immediately discard all the grey digits, and infer that the first digit of your PIN is either 1, 3, 4, 5, or 6. The digit's colors will then change, and by repeating the process a few times the machine will identify your first digit with certainty before carrying on with the second one.

Entering a code yourself is the best way to understand. Try entering 1234 or any PIN of your choice below in Interaction 1.

Congratulation! You now understand the principle of interaction with this interface. We describe next what you should expect to learn in this article.

Overview

In Interaction 1, we conveyed our intent to the machine via two big brightly colored buttons. The colors indicate what the buttons mean to both the machine and the user. This ability to translate button presses into their meanings seems essential for any interface to work.

In this article, we question this assumptions and ask: could a machine respond to user commands without knowing what those commands mean? Concretely, could our interface identify your PIN without knowing the colors of the buttons in advance? We showcase and decipher such a machine below using different versions of our interface. The plan is as follow:

  1. In section 1, we analyse the interface as presented in Interaction 1 and identify the various components at play.
  2. In section 2, we present the self-calibrating version of our interface. The buttons loose their pre-assigned colors. You will decide the buttons' colors in your mind and use them as such. The machine will nonetheless figure out your PIN, as well as the color of each button. We will decipher how it works and refine our mental model to include what we learned.
  3. In section 3, we scale our approach to continuous user's actions. A challenging task because it requires to train a classifier without access to known labels. To showcase this, our interface will no longer have buttons. You will place points on a 2D map and decide which areas are associated to which colors.
  4. In section 4, we demonstrate the use of richer interaction modalities. You will enter your PIN by drawing small sketches and using spoken commands. Once again, you will get to decide what sketches or sounds you want to use, and the machine will identify both your PIN and a classifier mapping your actions to their meanings.
  5. In section 5, we present previous academic work on the subject, position this work into the human-computer interface and machine-learning landscape, highlight some remaining challenges, and ask ourselves how this work can be useful with concrete examples from brain-computer interfaces to the study of language formation.
